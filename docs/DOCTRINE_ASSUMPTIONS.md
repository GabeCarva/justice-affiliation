# Doctrine Assumptions

For each doctrine, this document explains the inclusion rationale, what we assume about principled
adherents, the strongest counterargument, and sources consulted.

---

## Executive Restraint

**Why included:** Executive power limits are one of the most contested areas of constitutional law,
and party positions have clearly shifted — Republicans historically favored executive limits when
Democrats held the presidency, and vice versa. This creates numerous high-signal diagnostic cases.

**What we assume:** A principled adherent of executive restraint believes courts should block
executive orders and agency actions that exceed statutory or constitutional authority, regardless
of which party controls the executive. The doctrine applies symmetrically.

**Strongest counterargument:** The scope of executive power is genuinely debated. Some originalists
argue the Constitution vests broad "executive power" in the President (the unitary executive theory),
making aggressive executive action doctrinally acceptable. Under this view, upholding executive power
is consistent with originalist doctrine — not a departure from it.

**Sources:** Morrison v. Olson (1988), Youngstown Sheet & Tube Co. v. Sawyer (1952), INS v. Chadha
(1983), and academic work by Elena Kagan ("Presidential Administration," 2001), Steven Calabresi and
Christopher Yoo (The Unitary Executive, 2008).

## Congressional Spending Authority

**Why included:** The "power of the purse" — Congress's exclusive authority over federal appropriations
— is a structural feature of the Constitution that both originalists and textualists should enforce
consistently. Cases where the executive spends or withholds money contrary to congressional direction
are diagnostic.

**What we assume:** A principled adherent believes the Appropriations Clause (Art. I, § 9, cl. 7) requires
the executive to spend what Congress appropriates and not spend what Congress has not appropriated. Courts
should enforce this constraint symmetrically regardless of which party controls Congress or the executive.

**Strongest counterargument:** "Impoundment" — the executive's ability to refuse to spend appropriated
funds — has a long historical practice. Some presidents of both parties have used it. The Constitution
may not require immediate spending of every appropriated dollar.

**Sources:** Train v. City of New York (1975), Congressional Budget and Impoundment Control Act of 1974,
and Biden v. Nebraska (2023) for loan-forgiveness programs funded without appropriation.

## Federalism / States' Rights

**Why included:** Federalism doctrine — protecting state authority from federal encroachment — was
historically a conservative commitment, but has been selectively applied. When state action aligns
with federal conservative policy goals, federalism arguments often disappear; when it aligns with
liberal goals, they reappear.

**What we assume:** A principled federalism adherent applies the doctrine symmetrically — protecting
state authority from federal overreach regardless of whether the state policy aligns with their
political preferences.

**Strongest counterargument:** Some argue federalism properly serves as a floor against federal
oppression, not a ceiling on federal power to protect rights. Under this view, the federal government
overriding state resistance to civil rights protections is consistent with the Constitution's post-Civil
War amendments.

**Sources:** United States v. Lopez (1995), United States v. Morrison (2000), NFIB v. Sebelius (2012),
Murphy v. NCAA (2018), and Erwin Chemerinsky's Federal Jurisdiction.

## Agency Deference

**Why included:** The shift from Chevron deference (1984) to the post-Loper Bright framework (2024) is
one of the most politically significant doctrine shifts of the current era. Chevron was historically
supported by both parties when they controlled the executive. Republicans abandoned it after Democrats
consistently controlled executive agencies during the Obama years.

**What we assume:** The principled conservative position favors independent judicial review over
agency deference; the principled liberal position favors deference to agency expertise. But the test
is whether these positions are held consistently — a conservative who favored deference under Republican
administrations but opposes it under Democratic ones is not being principled.

**Strongest counterargument:** Chevron deference was itself a policy choice, not a constitutional
requirement. Abandoning it in favor of independent judicial interpretation is a legitimate doctrinal
development, not a partisan shift. Loper Bright's majority makes precisely this argument.

**Sources:** Chevron v. NRDC (1984), Kisor v. Wilkie (2019), Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo (2024),
Corner Post v. Board of Governors (2024), Garland v. Cargill (2024).

## Standing and Justiciability

**Why included:** Standing requirements determine who can challenge government action. Their strict or
loose enforcement has significant consequences for which cases courts can decide. Party positions on
standing have shifted based on which cases they want courts to be able to hear.

**What we assume:** A principled standing adherent enforces standing requirements consistently —
neither expanding them to allow preferred challengers nor contracting them to block disfavored cases.
The same standards should apply regardless of the subject matter.

**Strongest counterargument:** Some argue that the Constitution's "cases or controversies" requirement
is itself ambiguous, leaving courts discretion to calibrate standing based on separation-of-powers
concerns. Stricter standing in some contexts may reflect structural rather than partisan reasoning.

**Sources:** Lujan v. Defenders of Wildlife (1992), TransUnion v. Ramirez (2021), FDA v. Alliance for
Hippocratic Medicine (2024), Murthy v. Missouri (2024).

## Presidential Immunity

**Why included:** The question of presidential immunity from criminal prosecution reached the Supreme
Court directly in Trump v. United States (2024). The doctrine has minimal historical precedent and
strong originalist arguments on both sides.

**What we assume:** There is genuine constitutional ambiguity. We assume that a principled adherent
would apply the same immunity principles regardless of which party's president benefits.

**Strongest counterargument:** Trump v. United States is a nearly unique case. There is almost no
comparable historical precedent. Scoring it as "partisan" because a justice appointed by the president
facing prosecution voted for broad immunity may simply be an error of selection — a justice might
sincerely believe in broad immunity as an originalist matter.

**Sources:** Clinton v. Jones (1997), Nixon v. Fitzgerald (1982), Trump v. United States (2024).

## Nationwide Injunctions

**Why included:** The power of single district court judges to issue injunctions blocking federal
policy nationwide is a procedural doctrine with significant partisan consequences. The party in power
wants to limit it; the party out of power benefits from it.

**What we assume:** A principled adherent of limits on nationwide injunctions believes the remedy
should be applied consistently — limiting it regardless of which party's policy is being blocked.

**Strongest counterargument:** The doctrine of nationwide injunctions is genuinely unsettled.
Reasonable judges disagree about whether single-judge nationwide relief has any historical basis.
A judge who limits all nationwide injunctions regardless of party may simply be correct on the
procedural merits.

**Sources:** Trump v. Hawaii (2018), Biden v. Texas (2022), DHS v. New York (2020), and academic
work by Samuel Bray ("Multiple Chancellors," 2017).
